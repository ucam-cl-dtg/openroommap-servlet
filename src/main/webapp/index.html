OpenRoomMap

We want to collect a detailed indoor plan of the Computer Laboratory and we think that this information is best entered and kept up to date by building users themselves. Please use the link below to launch our editor software which you can use to position furniture and appliances within the building.

We are interested in as much detail as you are prepared to provide: the names of the floorboxes in your office, the location of air vents, the name of your workstation and the position of your desk and shelving.

Motivation

Pedestrian navigation research

This work uses inertial sensors (accelerometers and gyroscopes) to estimate the position of people within a building. The system combines updates from these sensors with a map of the building in order to constrain the location estimates and derive an accurate location. Problems remain with this technique in particular with movement in large rooms or for repeated small movements (such as when sat at a desk or giving a presentation). We are interested in whether constraints provided by information about the position of furniture within the building can help mitigate this problem.

Explaining energy consumption

The William Gates Building consumes a maximum of around 300kW during a busy weekday. Around 200kW of this is base-load which building consumption never drops below regardless of building usage, whether its a weekend, or the week after Christmas. We are interested in how to explain this consumption. One means to explain a building's electricity consumption is to do sub-metering and we could go as far as installing meters on every light and every desktop. However, this is expensive and the energy consumption of the metering itself needs to be considered too. We think that actually the majority of the energy consumers within the building are static (or predictable) and so we want to see if we can construct an explanation for the building's consumption based on an accurate inventory.

For more information contact Andrew Rice

Building services

It is relatively straightforward to find out the name of a workstation or server that has failed. A (manual) entry in the machine database can then be consulted to work out which room the machine is in. However, for large offices or teaching rooms this still leaves an element of manual searching required. An alternative is to consult the internal wiring database for this building, coupled with information from switches, and work out which floorbox the machine is attached to. For this to be effective we need to know where the floorboxes are. There are numerous other aspects of building use which would be useful to collect in future. Examples include the occupant's perception of the building temperature, lighting levels, mobile phone coverage, wireless network coverage.

Long-term research goal

The OpenStreetMap project is a highly successful initiative which is building a free, open map of the world. The map is constructed by volunteers who hand edit and annotate GPS traces of their journeys. We are seeing whether it is possible to build similar infrastructure for maintaining maps and plans of the interior of buildings. For large organisations this data would be useful for measuring inventories; keeping databases of office occupants, phone numbers and room use updated; for explaining energy consumption and targetting reductions; and for generating maps for visitors and meetings.

The application we have written for this study is a first step towards understanding this problem. We hope to get some insight and feedback from users of the William Gates Building to help us improve it.

Other research issues which we forsee include

  • Entering data for the internals of a building is a different operation to external mapping. In particular it is a constraint based problem. If you move a wall when updating a building plan this has an impact on all the rooms which share that wall and the contents of those rooms. Similarly, when placing objects within a room, users are more likely to give relative positions such as the desk is in the corner, the shelves are above the desk etc.
  • How to deal with privacy, confidentiality or security of detailed internal data. Possible approaches might be a distributed database of room contents which automatically contribute to centralised aggregate totals.
  • What incentives are needed for users to make data updates. Projects such as OpenStreetMap seem to attract contributors through no direct incentive but maybe indirect community effects are playing a part.
  • How to distribute updates and manage conflicts? The OpenStreetMap database is a single snap-shot in time. It doesn't describe historical data. However, historical information is important for OpenRoomMap in a number of situations. Firstly, analysing historical electricity meter data (for example) requires the historical state of the building. Secondly, distributing updates to the map requires that it must be easy to find out what has changed from a particular point in time.
